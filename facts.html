Alzheimer's Disease. What is it?

Alzheimer's disease is an irreversible brain disorder that progressively affects cognitive functioning such as thinking, remembering, and daily function activities. It is often confused with age-related dementia and has often been termed “Old-Timers”, but they are, in fact, not the same. Dementia may be a symptom of Alzheimer’s, but is not age-related, although it is a risk factor. Truth is scientists are still learning about Alzheimer’s Disease. The cause is unknown – it could be hereditary, environmental, and/or lifestyle.

It was first termed in 1906 by Dr. Alois Alzheimer after examining the brain tissue of a women who had symptoms of memory loss and hallucinations. Though only 55 years old at the time of her death, her brain tissue showed great abnormalities. He found clumps of amyloid plaques and tangled bundles of fibers (tau tangles). We now know these are some of the features of Alzheimer’s disease.

Alzheimer's Disease. Signs and Symptoms

Sometimes the changes affecting the brain may actually happen a decade before signs and symptoms of Alzheimer's may appear. Symptoms may vary from person to person, but the most common symptom is memory loss - forgetting to take medication, forgetting important dates, and repeating questions are common. Problem solving challenges may also present itself with Alzheimer's Disease. Following a recipe may become more difficult, as well as handling the financial affairs such as paying bills on time. Confusion is also often seen with Alzheimer's Disease. Losing track of time, date, and seasons may become more frequent with the progression of the disease. Some may notice mood changes with Alzheimer's Disease. Suspicion, agitation, fear, and anxiety can often be seen with someone dealing with Alzheimer's.

Signs of Alzheimer's Disease include:

  • Memory loss
  • Confusion
  • Agitation and other mood changes
  • Declining problem-solving skills
  • Difficulty with finances and timely bill payment
  • Repeating questions
  • Misplacing things
  • Forgetting dates and losing track of time and seasons
  • Wandering

Alzheimer's Disease. Stages.

There are three categories of Alzheimer's Disease - mild, moderate, and severe.

  • Mild Alzheimer's Disease. This is the early stage of Alzheimer's Disease. A person with mild Alzheimer's may still function normally and conduct normal activity, but may find themselves having a hard time trying to find the right word or name, losing and misplacing objects, and begin having issues with organization.
  • Moderate Alzheimer's Disease. This is the middle stage of Alzheimer's Disease. A person with Alzheimer's in moderate stage may have more pronuounced symptoms. They may be more forgetful, more withdrawn and moody, and more confused to time and place.
  • Severe Alzheimer's Disease. This is the late stage of Alzheimer's Disease. Symptoms are severe. A person with Alzheimer's in late stage may have difficulty interacting with their environment. Conversations and communication are difficult. Physical abilities are decreased. A person with severe Alzheimer's will require around the clock care.

Each stage of Alzheimer's is different for each person. Stages may often overlap and vary. Alzheimer's changes can affect the brain long before symptoms appear.
