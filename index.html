Alzheimer's Disease and Dementia

Alzheimer's disease is a degenerative brain disease in which symptoms often get worse with time. As the disease progresses, nerve cells become damaged or destroyed and often the patient loses the ability to carry out daily activity functions such as walking, swallowing, and critical thinking.

As the elderly population grows, the number of Alzheimer's patients grow as well. By 2050, it is estimated the number of people 65 years and older is projected to reach 13.8 million.

Dementia is not a specific disease, but is a general term to describbe memory loss along with other impairment such as forgetfulness and loss of problem-solving skills and activities of daily function.

Facts

According to the Alzheimer's Association

  • More than 5 million Americans are living with Alzheimer's Disease
  • Alzheimer's disease is the 6th leading cause of death in the United States
  • Alzheimer's accounts for 60-80% of the most common cause of dementia

Caregivers

Caring for someone with Alzheimer's Disease can feel overwhelming and stressful. A caregiver for someone with Alzheimer's typically care for someone for a longer duration when compared to someone with other conditions. The disease is progressive and may progress over many years. According to the CDC, 57% of family caregivers care for a loved one with Alzheimer's for over four years.
